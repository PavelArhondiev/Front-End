Mountain – top observatory sees Gamma rays from exotic Milky Way object

For the first time, an international collaboration of scientists, including physicists from the University of Utah, has detected highly energetic light coming from the outermost regions of an unusual star system within our own galaxy. The source is a microquasar—a black hole that gobbles up matter from a nearby companion star and blasts out powerful jets of material. The team’s observations, described in the issue of the journal Nature, strongly suggest that electron acceleration and collisions at the ends of the microquasar’s jets produced the powerful gamma rays. Scientists think that studying messengers from this microquasar may offer a glimpse into more extreme events happening at the centers of distant galaxies.

New research about Martian moons contradicts long-held theory

The two moons that orbit Mars are likely made up of chunks of the planet itself, contradicting a long-standing theory that the moons, Phobos and Deimos, were asteroids captured in Mars’ gravitational pull, a new study suggests. University of Alberta geologist Chris Herd, who was a co-author of the study, used reflective spectroscopy to compare a sample of the Tagish Lake meteorite to Phobos and Deimos.
